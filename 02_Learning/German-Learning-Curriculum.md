# German Learning Curriculum for Deaf Learners

*Standard German → Austrian German | Reading/Writing Focus | No Audio Required*

---

## Philosophy: Visual-First Language Acquisition

As a deaf learner, you have an **advantage** most hearing learners lack: you're not distracted by pronunciation anxiety or listening comprehension gaps. You can focus entirely on:
- **Grammar structures**
- **Vocabulary patterns**
- **Reading comprehension**
- **Written expression**

This approach actually mirrors how many hearing people learn classical languages (Latin, Ancient Greek) — through texts, not speech.

### Multilingual Advantage

You're already **quadrilingual**: English (fluent), ASL (fluent/native), French (some), Spanish (some). This changes everything:

**Transfer Learning Benefits:**
- **French → German:** Cognates ("nation" → *Nation*, "information" → *Information*), grammatical gender concepts, formal register awareness
- **Spanish → German:** Case system familiarity (Spanish has remnants), verb conjugation patterns, Romance vocabulary base
- **English → German:** Massive cognate overlap ("house" → *Haus*, "water" → *Wasser*, "friend" → *Freund*)
- **ASL → German:** Strong visual processing, spatial grammar understanding (German word order is spatially logical), comfort with non-English syntax

**Revised Timeline:**
- Monolingual learner: 12 months to B1 reading
- **You: 6-8 months to B1 reading** (accelerated by language learning experience)
- Can skip "baby steps" phases, move straight to structured grammar + reading

**What This Means:**
- German gender system (der/die/das) — you already handle this in French/Spanish
- Case system — new, but your brain is used to grammatical complexity
- Compound words (*Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaftskapitän*) — ASL prepares you for complex visual structures
- Word order — challenging, but you're used to non-English syntax from ASL

## Leveraging Your Languages: Transfer Strategy

### English → German (Cognate Treasure)

English and German are **siblings** (both Germanic). You'll recognize:
- **House** → *Haus*
- **Water** → *Wasser*
- **Friend** → *Freund*
- **Book** → *Buch*
- **Hand** → *Hand*
- **Finger** → *Finger*

**Strategy:** When reading, guess first. 60-70% of basic German vocabulary has English cognates.

### French → German (Romance Contributions)

French influenced German heavily (especially Austrian German, which was part of Habsburg territories):
- **Nation** → *Nation* (feminine in both)
- **Information** → *Information*
- **Restaurant** → *Restaurant*
- **Garage** → *Garage*

**Austrian bonus:** Many Austrian food terms come via French (Austrian court culture):
- *Tasse* (cup) ← French "tasse"
- *Serviette* (napkin) ← French "serviette"

### Spanish → German (Grammar Bridge)

Both have:
- **Grammatical gender** (el/la → der/die/das)
- **Verb conjugation** (though German is simpler)
- **Formal/informal distinction** (*Sie/du* like *usted/tú*)

**Strategy:** Apply Spanish gender intuition (often matches: *la mano* → *die Hand*, but watch for exceptions)

### ASL → German (Visual Processing)

ASL's **spatial grammar** prepares you for German word order:
- ASL: Time + Topic + Comment + Verb
- German: Time + Manner + Place + Verb (at end)

Both require **holding information** while the sentence unfolds.

## Phase 1: Reactivating High School German (Weeks 1-4)

### Goals
- Reactivate dormant vocabulary
- Rebuild basic grammar foundations
- Establish daily reading habit

### Materials (All Visual/Text-Based)

**1. Memrise (German Course) — RECOMMENDED STARTER**
- Strength: Better spaced repetition than Duolingo, authentic video clips with native speakers (visual context), faster progression
- Focus: Vocabulary + natural phrases
- Time: 15-20 min/day
- Note: More respectful of adult learners, less "gamified nonsense"
- Alternative: Duolingo (if you prefer the game format, but Memrise is more efficient)

**2. "German Made Simple" by Arnold Leitner**
- Format: Traditional textbook, grammar explanations
- Strength: Clear grammar rules, reading passages, written exercises
- Approach: Work through 1-2 chapters/week

**3. Nico's Weg (DW - Deutsche Welle)**
- Format: Interactive online course with transcripts
- URL: learngerman.dw.com
- Strength: Written transcripts for all video content
- Focus: Read transcripts, complete written exercises

**4. Clozemaster (Month 2+) — ESSENTIAL FOR YOUR LEVEL**
- Format: Fill-in-the-blank sentences from authentic German
- Strength: Contextual learning, real sentences, not artificial drills
- Free tier: Very usable
- When to start: After basic vocabulary reactivation (Week 3-4)

### Daily Routine (30-45 min)
- **Morning:** Memrise vocabulary (15 min) — reactivate high school German
- **Evening:** Textbook chapter or Nico's Weg lesson (20-30 min) — structured grammar
- **Weekend:** Review vocabulary flashcards (Anki deck) — long-term retention

**Alternative:** If you prefer more challenge, swap Memrise for Clozemaster after Week 2

## Phase 2: Building Reading Fluency (Weeks 5-12)

### Goals
- Read simple German texts without translation
- Master core grammar (cases, verb conjugations, word order)
- Build vocabulary to ~2,000 words

### Materials

**1. Graded Readers (Stufe 1-2)**
- "Learn German With Stories" by André Klein (series)
  - *Café in Berlin* (A1/A2)
  - *Ferien in Frankfurt* (A2)
  - *Karneval in Köln* (A2/B1)
- Strength: Simple prose, vocabulary lists, comprehension questions
- Approach: Read without dictionary first, mark unknown words, then review

**2. Deutsche Welle (DW) News - "Langsam gesprochene Nachrichten"**
- Format: News articles with transcripts
- Strength: Current events, clear writing, transcripts provided
- Note: Ignore the "slowly spoken" aspect — focus on written text
- URL: dw.com/deutsch-lernen

**3. Grammar Reference: "Hammer's German Grammar and Usage"**
- Format: Comprehensive reference (not textbook)
- Use: Look up concepts as you encounter them
- Strength: Detailed explanations of case system, verb patterns

### Daily Routine (45-60 min)
- **Graded Reader:** 1 chapter/day (20 min)
- **DW Article:** 1 news article with note-taking (20 min)
- **Grammar Review:** Specific concept study (15 min)
- **Vocabulary:** Anki flashcards (10 min)

## Phase 3: Authentic Materials (Months 4-6)

### Goals
- Read authentic German texts (novels, newspapers, websites)
- Understand complex sentence structures
- Express yourself in writing

### Materials

**1. Children/YA Literature**
- *Die unendliche Geschichte* (The Neverending Story) — Michael Ende
- *Tintenherz* (Inkheart) — Cornelia Funke
- *Emil und die Detektive* — Erich Kästner (classic)
- Approach: Read with dictionary nearby, but try to infer meaning first

**2. German Newspapers (Online)**
- **Die Zeit** — thoughtful, longer articles
- **Spiegel Online** — current events
- **Süddeutsche Zeitung** — quality journalism
- Start with culture/lifestyle sections, move to politics

**3. Wikipedia (German)**
- Read about familiar topics in German
- Compare English and German versions
- Note technical vocabulary patterns

**4. Writing Practice**
- **Lang-8** or **HiNative** — native speakers correct your writing
- Keep a daily journal in German (even simple sentences)
- Write summaries of articles you've read

## Phase 4: Austrian German Specialization (Months 6-12)

### Goals
- Recognize Austrian vocabulary and expressions
- Read Austrian media confidently
- Understand cultural context

### Key Differences (Standard → Austrian)

| Standard German | Austrian German | English |
|----------------|-----------------|---------|
| die Tomate | die Paradeiser | tomato |
| die Kartoffel | die Erdapfel | potato |
| das Brot | die Semmel | bread roll |
| die Sahne | der Obers | cream |
| der Kühlschrank | der Eisschrank | refrigerator |
| grüß Gott | grüß Gott | hello (Austrian) |
| Tschüss | Servus | goodbye/hello (regional) |

### Materials for Austrian German

**1. Austrian Newspapers**
- **Der Standard** — Vienna-based, quality journalism
- **Die Presse** — conservative, business focus
- **Kurier** — tabloid, everyday language

**2. Austrian Literature**
- **Thomas Bernhard** — playwright, novelist (challenging but rewarding)
- **Elfriede Jelinek** — Nobel Prize winner (complex)
- **Stefan Zweig** — classic Austrian writer (more accessible)
- **Daniel Kehlmann** — contemporary, readable

**3. Austrian Blogs/Websites**
- Search for Austrian food blogs, travel sites
- Look for ".at" domains (Austrian websites)

**4. "Wörterbuch Deutsch-Österreichisch"**
- Dictionary of Austrian German
- Reference when encountering unfamiliar terms

### Cultural Context Resources

**Books:**
- "The Austrians: A Thousand-Year Odyssey" — Gordon Brook-Shepherd
- "Austrian History" sections in general German history books

**Online:**
- ORF (Austrian Broadcasting) website — news, culture

## Grammar Priorities (Reading Comprehension Focus)

### Essential (Learn First)
1. **Cases** (Nominative, Accusative, Dative, Genitive)
   - Critical for understanding sentence structure
   - Focus on article forms (der/die/das, den/die/das, etc.)

2. **Verb Conjugation**
   - Present tense (all regular and common irregular)
   - Past tenses (Perfekt for conversation, Präteritum for literature)

3. **Word Order**
   - Verb position rules (V2 in main clauses, final position in subordinate)
   - This is crucial for reading comprehension

4. **Modal Verbs** (können, müssen, wollen, sollen, dürfen, mögen)

### Important (Learn Next)
5. **Adjective Endings** (agreement with case/gender)
6. **Prepositions** + which case they govern
7. **Separable Verbs**
8. **Passive Voice** (more common in formal writing)

### Advanced (For Literature)
9. **Subjunctive (Konjunktiv II)** — hypothetical statements
10. **Extended Adjective Constructions** — complex descriptive phrases

## Austrian German-Specific Notes

### Vocabulary Categories to Focus On

**Food & Dining** (Major differences)
- Learn Austrian food terms early if interested in culinary topics
- Many Austrian terms derive from Czech, Hungarian, Italian influences

**Politics & Administration**
- Austria has different political party names
- Administrative vocabulary differs (states vs. federal)

**Everyday Objects**
- Household items often have different names
- Shopping vocabulary important for daily life

### Regional Variation
Austrian German varies by region:
- **Vienna** — most "standard" Austrian German
- **Tyrol/Salzburg** — more Bavarian influence
- **Vorarlberg** — Swiss German influence (very different)

For reading, **Vienna-based media** is your best bet for consistent Austrian German.

## App Comparison for Your Situation

### Memrise vs. Duolingo

| Feature | Memrise | Duolingo |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **Repetition** | Smarter spaced repetition | Repetitive, sometimes annoying |
| **Authentic content** | Native speaker video clips | Cartoonish illustrations |
| **Progression** | Faster, respects adult learners | Slow, treats everyone like beginners |
| **German quality** | Better, more natural phrases | Sometimes weird sentences |
| **Free tier** | Generous | Full course free, but with ads |
| **Best for** | People with some background | True beginners |

**Verdict:** You're not a true beginner. Use Memrise (or skip apps entirely and go straight to graded readers + textbook).

### Why Clozemaster Beats Both (Month 2+)

Once you have 500+ words reactivated, **Clozemaster** becomes the most efficient tool:
- Real German sentences, not artificial drills
- Forces you to think in context
- Faster progression
- Free tier is fully functional

**Your workflow:**
- **Weeks 1-2:** Memrise (reactivation)
- **Week 3+:** Clozemaster (authentic context) + graded readers
- **Month 2+:** Drop apps, focus on reading + Anki

## Final Notes

### Advantages of Your Approach
1. **No accent anxiety** — focus purely on comprehension
2. **Strong visual memory** — text-based learning plays to your strengths
3. **Deep grammatical understanding** — not distracted by conversation shortcuts
4. **Literary focus possible** — many deaf German readers excel at literature

### Challenges to Anticipate
1. **Idioms/Colloquialisms** — harder to learn without hearing context
   - *Solution:* Focus on formal/written registers first
2. **Dialect Variation** — Austrian German has many spoken dialects
   - *Solution:* Stick to written standard Austrian German (newspapers, literature)
3. **Slang/Informal Language** — evolves rapidly, harder to track
   - *Solution:* Learn formal register first, add slang later

### The Austrian German Timeline
- **Months 1-3:** Standard German reactivation (A2 level reading) — accelerated due to French/Spanish background
- **Months 3-6:** Standard German (B1 level reading) — leverage English cognates
- **Months 4-6:** Introduce Austrian vocabulary alongside standard
- **Months 6-9:** Comfortable with Austrian texts, recognize regional patterns

**Note:** Your multilingual background means you can compress the beginner phase. Focus on:
1. **False friends** (German "also" = "therefore," not "also")
2. **Case system** (the big new concept)
3. **Word order rules** (different from both English and Romance languages)

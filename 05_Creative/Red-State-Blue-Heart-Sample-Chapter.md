# RED STATE, BLUE HEART

*A Modern Romance*

## Chapter One: The Rally

**Romeo** 

The first thing Romeo noticed about the girl was her sign.

It was terrible. Hand-lettered in purple marker, the cardboard sagged in the December drizzle, bleeding *"HOUSING IS A HUMAN RIGHT"* into something that looked like a prophecy from a depressed octopus.

She stood two rows ahead of him in the crowd gathered outside City Hall, shivering in a thrifted wool coat that had seen better decades, combat boots planted on cracked concrete like she was ready to fight God Himself for the cause of affordable rent control.

Romeo adjusted his own sign—professionally printed, *"TAX THE RICH—BUT FAIRLY"*—and wondered what the hell he was doing here.

He knew exactly what he was doing here. He was maintaining cover.

His father thought he was at Young Republicans happy hour. His mother believed he was canvassing for the special election. His brothers—the three of them, lined up like Russian nesting dolls of toxic masculinity—assumed he was at the shooting range, practicing to protect their hypothetical future families from the inevitable liberal apocalypse.

Instead, he was freezing his admittedly well-developed ass off at a Democratic Socialists rally, surrounded by people who would have his family on a pyre if they knew his last name.

The Carters of Dallas—oil money, old money, *Republican* money. Three generations of conservative values, golf club memberships, and perfectly maintained plausible deniability. Romeo's grandfather had literally whispered policy into governors' ears. His father still did.

And Romeo... Romeo was the malfunctioning heir.

"*The socialist alternative!*" The speaker on the makeshift stage was shouting into a microphone that squealed feedback every third word. "*When corporations own politicians, the people—*" *SCREEECH* "*—must own the means of production!*"

Romeo winced. The crowd cheered. The girl with the bleeding sign cheered harder, throwing her fist into the air, and in the motion her coat gaped open to reveal a t-shirt that read: *"EAT THE RICH—NO REALLY, THEY'RE MARBLIZED."*

He laughed.

He couldn't help it. It was ridiculous and clever and he was so tired of pretending to believe that tax cuts for billionaires would eventually trickle down to anyone who wasn't already drinking from a platinum fire hose.

The laugh drew her attention.

She turned—dark eyes, darker hair escaping from a messy bun, a nose ring that caught the streetlight like a challenge—and Romeo felt something shift in his chest. Not lust, though she was objectively gorgeous in a *'I haven't slept in 48 hours organizing a mutual aid network'* kind of way. Something worse.

Recognition.

She saw him. Really saw him—Argentinian mother, blue-eyed tower of a father, the exact genetic dice-roll that made him look like he should be captaining a yacht in some tax haven while sneering at the poors. She saw the expensive wool coat his mother had bought him, the leather boots, the haircut that cost more than her rent.

Her expression hardened. She turned back to the speaker.

*Good*, Romeo thought. *She should hate me. It's safer for both of us.*

But then the speaker finished, the crowd began to disperse, and Romeo found himself walking toward her instead of toward the parking lot where his respectable sedan waited like a guilty secret.

"Your sign's bleeding," he said.

She turned again, wary this time. "It's raining. Cardboard does that."

"No, I mean—" He gestured at the purple prophecy. "The letters. They're running. Like mascara after a funeral."

She stared at him. "Is that... a compliment?"

"It's an observation. I'm good at those. Terrible at most other things."

She almost smiled. He saw it—the tug at the corner of her mouth, quickly suppressed. "You're overdressed for a socialist rally."

"I'm underdressed for my own inescapable doom. This is the compromise."

That got her. She laughed—a real one, surprised out of her, and Romeo felt it like a physical thing, warm in the cold evening.

"Juliet," she said, sticking out her hand. "With a sign that will obviously survive the revolution."

He took it. Her fingers were cold and strong and callused at the tips, probably from the very specific brutality of hand-lettering dozens of signs for various doomed causes.

"Romeo," he said. "And I promise I'm not here to infiltrate anything."

"Could be exactly what an infiltrator would say."

"True. But I'd have a better sign."

She looked at his professionally printed board. "*Tax the Rich—But Fairly?* That's the most Republican DSA sign I've ever seen."

"It's the trauma," he said, and something in his tone made her pause. "I was raised by wolves. Wealthy ones. They're harder to kill than you'd think."

The drizzle was turning to rain. People were leaving, pulling up hoods, heading toward warm apartments and enthusiastic debates about Palestine and podcasts. Juliet should have been one of them.

"There's a bar," she said, not quite looking at him. "Around the corner. They have a drink called *The Means of Production*. It's mostly whiskey and rage."

"Is that an invitation?"

"It's a test. Infiltrators usually order wine."

"I hate wine. It reminds me of my father."

She met his eyes then, searching. "What's your actual damage, Romeo? Because you're either the most committed long-con artist I've ever met or you're just... sad."

"Can I be both?"

"You can buy the first round and we'll see."

They walked together through the December rain, not touching, not quite looking at each other, Romeo wondering if he was making the biggest mistake of his life or the first honest choice he'd made in years.

His phone buzzed in his pocket. A text from his brother, Tybalt: *Where are you? Dad wants to talk about the campaign strategy. It's war, brother. They're coming for everything.* 

Romeo silenced the notification without reading it.

Beside him, Juliet was texting someone too, her thumbs flying—probably warning her friends that she was about to get a drink with a suspiciously well-groomed class traitor.

"My parents," she said, not looking up, "are currently at a $500-a-plate fundraiser for displaced bees. They think they're radicals. They have a *gardener*."

Romeo laughed. "My parents think tax breaks for billionaires create jobs. They have three houses and a compound in Montana for when society collapses."

"So we're both disasters."

"I'm a disaster with good bone structure and a trust fund I haven't touched in two years. You're a disaster with..." He gestured at her sign, now officially unreadable. "...revolutionary artistic vision."

"Trust fund?" She stopped walking. "Okay, full disclosure. I have three roommates. Two jobs. Zero savings. And despite all the righteous anger, I still think Felicity is the best TV show created in the 90s."

"Never seen it."

"We can't be friends."

"I'm only interested in enemies anyway. Less pressure."

She studied him. The rain was soaking through his expensive coat and she was shivering in her vintage wool and neither of them moved to solve either problem.

"The bar," she said finally. "Five minutes. If you can explain why you're really here without using the words 'economic anxiety' or suggesting that crypto will save us, I'll consider not warning every person in this neighborhood that you're a potential fed."

"Deal. But you have to explain the bee thing."

"The bees are the only pure thing my parents care about," she said, starting to walk again. "They're performative radicals. All the aesthetic, none of the praxis."

"And you?"

"I have terrible signs and no patience for bullshit."

"That's not an ideology, Juliet."

"It's the only one that matters."

They reached the bar—a narrow, dark place with socialist-realist murals and a bartender who looked like he could quote theory for hours or break a bottle over someone's head, depending on the vibe. They took a booth in the back, Juliet sliding in first, Romeo following, still wondering what the hell he was doing.

She ordered the Means of Production.

He ordered bourbon. Straight.

"Okay," she said, leaning forward, elbows on the sticky table, *okay*, she was beautiful up close, the kind of beautiful that was actually just *alive-ness*, intensity, someone who hadn't numbed themselves into acceptance of how terrible everything was. "Your turn. Why was Romeo at the DSA rally? And don't lie. I'm very good at knowing when men are lying. It's my superpower."

He looked at her—really looked—and made the choice. The first of many he would make over the next three months that would unravel his entire constructed life, thread by careful thread.

"Because," Romeo said, "if I have to pretend to care about estate tax reform for one more second, I will actually lose my mind. And you were the most honest thing I'd seen in years."

She blinked. "That's... that's actually a really good line."

"It's not a line."

"That's what every infiltrator says."

"I know."

"So how do I know you're real?"

Romeo reached across the table and took her cold hand in his, ignoring the way she tensed, ignoring the warning bells in his head, ignoring everything except the terrifying possibility that this—this impossible girl with her terrible sign and her sharp eyes and her complete rejection of everything he'd been taught to value—might be the only real thing he'd ever found.

"You don't," he said. "That's the risk."

She didn't pull away.

Outside, the rain kept falling. Inside, something dangerous and necessary was just beginning.

**[End Chapter One]**

## Working Hypothesis

The relational dynamic exhibits characteristics of an **anxious-avoidant attachment fusion with narcissistic supply cycling**, operating within a framework of **intermittent reinforcement** that maintains the narrator's emotional investment despite chronic ambiguity. The pattern is consistent with what attachment researchers call "disorganized attachment with approach-avoidance conflict"—simultaneous pursuit of intimacy and terror of vulnerability, managed through triangulation and control mechanisms.

The subject displays a **stable instability** pattern: the relationship cannot resolve into clear intimacy nor clean separation. Instead, it persists in a perpetual threshold state that serves the subject's need for narcissistic validation while avoiding the accountability of committed partnership.

---

## Evidence Supporting This Interpretation

### 1. Intermittent Reinforcement Schedule (The Core Binding Mechanism)

The timeline documents a precise operant conditioning pattern:
- **Closeness phases:** Extended hugs ("5 minutes or so"), intimate gifts with ritual presentation (bike with fairy lights, 5 cards), physical affection (cheek kisses, hand-holding, shoulder massage "for like 5 minutes straight"), love songs, notes saying "I love you"
- **Withdrawal phases:** Immediate post-intimacy shutdowns (post-Valentine's Day turtling, post-Florida trip emotional distance), unexplained silences, cruel manifestos, physical avoidance after her visits

**Why this matters:** Intermittent reinforcement creates stronger behavioral persistence than consistent reward. The narrator's confusion ("wondering if we were just hanging out as friends or not") isn't incidental—it's the structural output of a reward schedule designed to maximize uncertainty and, therefore, engagement.

### 2. Triangulation as Regulation Strategy

The girlfriend/ex-girlfriend functions not as a competing love interest but as a **regulatory object** that modulates intimacy distance:
- During girlfriend visits: Subject performs "perfect boyfriend" role, narrator is excluded/ignored
- Post-girlfriend visits: Subject resumes intensive pursuit of narrator (clingy behavior, requests for hangouts, notes, gifts)
- During breakup: Subject immediately pivots to deepened intimacy with narrator ("he seemed happier to be back rather than missing her")

**Critical evidence:** Subject explicitly told narrator he was "prioritising spending more time with her than with me" in May 2024—this communication served to create scarcity, not clarity. The girlfriend's presence allows the subject to approach without accountability; her absence allows approach without commitment.

### 3. Boundaries as Control Mechanisms

The subject's boundary violations are not random—they're **instrumental**:
- Physically blocking exits during arguments (July 2025)
- Monitoring narrator's social media (checking stories "every few seconds")
- Opening narrator's mail without notification
- Taking narrator's personal items (silk pillowcase, Neruda book, denim jacket) and keeping them as transitional objects
- The "no falling in love" contract imposed unilaterally, despite his own behavior contradicting this

**Key structural element:** The subject controls information flow—what the girlfriend knows, what the narrator knows, when and how contact occurs. This maintains the subject as the fulcrum of the relational triangle.

### 4. Physical Intimacy / Emotional Denial Split

The magnitude of physical and quasi-romantic behavior without verbal acknowledgment suggests **reality distortion** as a deliberate strategy:

**Direct evidence of intimacy:**
- Hand-holding initiated by subject "every time"
- Post-marathon hand and shoulder kisses
- Prolonged physical contact (leaning on narrator's chest while editing photos, hip-patting wake-up)
- Valentine's Day roses, kept for a year, petals carefully preserved after "accidentally" falling
- The "friend" card delivered after months of escalating intimacy

**Direct evidence of denial:**
- April 2025 note: "not in love with me, and always never will be"
- Consistent framing as "platonic roommates only" when pressed
- "Maybe your younger brother!" deflection

**Clinical interpretation:** This isn't confusion—it's **gaslighting by behavior**. The subject creates an unmistakable romantic environment, then denies its meaning when narrators seek clarity. This keeps the narrator in a state of cognitive dissonance ("I brought up the roses, the gift, the kisses, everything that no platonic friends would ever do") that serves the subject's need for validation without vulnerability.

### 5. Narcissistic Supply Cycling

The pattern of idealization and devaluation becomes pronounced in 2025:
- **Idealization:** Birthday manifestos about narrator's "essence," declarations that narrator is a "force of chaos" around which his life changes, obsessive attention during pursuit phases
- **Devaluation:** "Crazy" accusations, manifestos about narrator's childhood trauma and gender, "sob story" dismissal of financial hardship, callousness about employment loss

**The July 2025 rant** [INFERENCE: narcissistic injury response]: The paranoid content (accusing narrator of secret job and hidden savings), the sense of entitlement to narrator's whereabouts, and the rapid oscillation back to sweetness ("here's why it's so hard to connect with you") suggests narcissistic injury followed by hoovering behavior.

### 6. Object Constancy Deficits

The subject cannot maintain stable emotional representation of the narrator:
- Learns to "detach when I'm happy because he knew it would be temporary"
- Sudden emotional distance after periods of closeness (Florida trip, marathon day, Thanksgiving)
- The "turtling" pattern—retreat to room after intense intimacy

**Attachment reading:** This is consistent with avoidant attachment with anxious features—the subject desires connection but experiences it as engulfment, requiring periodic withdrawal to restore ego boundaries.

### 7. Saviour Complex Manifestation

The July manifesto about narrator's childhood trauma and whether subject could "help" represents **projective identification**—the subject positions himself as potential healer, not partner, maintaining hierarchical distance and avoiding reciprocal vulnerability.

## Addressing Alternative Interpretations

### Alternative 1: Genuine Confusion / Mutual Ambivalence

**Why this is less supported:**
- The power asymmetry is stark: Subject controls living situation, emotional tone, information flow, and physical boundaries. The narrator is consistently reactive ("freezing him out," "thawing," confusion). 
- The subject's behavior is **strategic**, not confused: hiding evidence of intimacy before girlfriend visits (removing notes from notepads, flipping notebook), the careful management of what each party knows, the sudden "platonic" contracts when narrator gets too close.
- A genuinely confused person doesn't produce elaborate gift rituals, preserve petals for a year, or send love songs while claiming platonic intent. They also don't physically block exits or monitor social media obsessively.

### Alternative 2: Autistic Social Navigation Difficulties

**Why this is less supported:**
- Subject self-diagnoses autism [direct text reference], but the claim is **contradicted by his own behavior**:
  - Perfect mirroring of narrator's body language and facial expressions during arguments (July 2025)
  - Precise reading of narrator's emotional states (noticing "balled up fists," facial expressions)
  - Sophisticated manipulation of social information (hiding notes, managing girlfriend's knowledge)
  - High social fluency with others ("bro mode" with friends, performative competence)
- [INFERENCE: The autism claim may function as plausible deniability for behavior that is otherwise clearly instrumental]

### Alternative 3: Fear of Coming Out / Internalized Homophobia

**Why this is less supported:**
- The subject discusses narrator's "sexuality and gender" with friends, jokes about narrator "working so hard not to be a gay boy," and generally treats the narrator's trans identity with casual familiarity—not shame.
- The pattern persists regardless of girlfriend's presence or absence. If this were simple fear of queer identity, we would expect less elaborate romantic staging (roses, bike gift, Valentine's gestures) and more consistent avoidance.
- The girlfriend functions as **enabler of intimacy**, not obstacle to it. The subject returns to the narrator immediately after breakups, not before them.

### Alternative 4: Polyamorous Orientation

**Why this is less supported:**
- No consent-based communication about multiple relationships. The subject hides information (didn't tell girlfriend about narrator's importance, hid notes before her visits).
- The "soulmate" declaration to girlfriend and simultaneous pursuit of narrator suggests **deception**, not ethical non-monogamy.
- The subject is possessive of narrator (jealousy, monitoring) without offering reciprocal transparency or commitment.

## Summary

This is not a relationship stuck in ambiguity—it is a relationship **structured by ambiguity**. The ambiguity is the product, not the problem. The subject requires the narrator's emotional investment without the accountability of commitment, and has developed a sophisticated behavioral repertoire to maintain this equilibrium. The narrator's repeated attempts to clarify the relationship are systematically subverted by the subject's withdrawal, denial, or strategic deployment of vulnerability.

The roses preserved for a year, the petals carefully gathered after falling, the silk pillowcase kept and washed repeatedly—these are not behaviors of a confused man. They are behaviors of a man who **knows exactly what he's doing**, even if he cannot say it.
